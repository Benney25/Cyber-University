Here are some basic tools you can use to help make your learning experience easier.


Firefox* & Chrome (Having both in addition to Microsoft Edge, Safari Browser, and other browsers, is essential for the web development track)

Libreoffice is basically the same as the Microsoft Office Suite, but it’s free. Some functionality differs, but I prefer it. Alternatively, you can use Google Docs, etc...

Calibre is my e-book reader of choice versus the Kindle browser-based reader. The Kindle browser-based reader relies on poor browser memory caching, which makes flipping pages take an eternity, and rely heavily on an internet connection and access to your Amazon account to access your expensive learning books, which is far from ideal. We all forget passwords, and not all of us have internet access at home. Learning should be easy, not a struggle. Buy ebooks you can keep and use offline, use Calibre.

Anki is the only effective spaced-repetition memorization software in the world, and is so much more effective than regular flashcards that it’s not even funny. Use it wisely though. Do your research and learn how to use it most effectively because this program can easily be used improperly and inefficiently.

VLC Media Player is the media player I use now as a replacement for the ever-buggy, ever-crashing Windows Media Player. It’s reliable, and has better features.

VSCode – OR – VSCodium* VSCode is a classic beginners IDE, but it belongs to Microsoft, and tends to push certain software and many privacy proponents speak out against using VSCode because Microsoft can collect your information through it.

Google Calendar is by far one of the most powerful and reliable calendars on the market to date, that I’m aware of. Given a little time figuring out the hotkeys/shortcuts, and you’re well on your way to becoming a time-management master.



If you’re looking for some preferred tools for getting your hands deep and dirty in tech related projects, here are some of my favorites.

Packet Tracer is a just-good-enough network simulation software for studying networking and practicing networking concepts. The next step-up I would have to say is CML, or Cisco Modeling Labs which costs $199/year, but provides complete virtualization support for networking devices and user operating systems alike.

Oracle VirtualBox and/or Vmware Workstation: We’re all going to need virtualization software for our learning journey.

GIT via Github or GitLAB*: These are tools for keeping track of versions of whatever it is. Maybe it’s software, usually it’s software. Maybe it’s a database, or it could be your personal ___. This is not to be treated as centralized, or cloud storage; because working on the same file in the same project at different locations can introduce version conflicts that can sometimes be impossible to resolve. For that, I would suggest learning how to set up network storage, or how to use cloud services to host your work. These are often paid, but hosting your own internet-facing cloud service can often be unsafe practice if you don’t know what you’re doing.

Cyber University is meant to be a one-stop reference for free, quality educational resources, for all. Equal Accessibility to quality, affordable education is our number one priority.


Cyber University was formed out of frustration with a swath of issues inherent in institutional education. Namely: unreasonably high tuition prices, low teacher quality, and how locked-down students are forced to be once they choose their teachers – if they can get a teacher at a time that works with their schedule at all and non-standard education between seemingly equivalent courses at adjacent schools.
